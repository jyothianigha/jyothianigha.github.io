3.2 Links and Associations

How individual objects connect to one another, and how those connections are generalized into a structural relationship at the class level.

3.2.1 General Concepts 3.2.2 Multiplicity 3.2.3 Importance of Associations
3.2.1

General Concepts

Objects rarely exist in isolation. A Person works for a Company; an Order is placed by a Customer; a Student enrolls in a Course. Object-oriented modeling gives these connections two related names, depending on the level at which we're looking at them.

Link A link is a physical or conceptual connection between two (or more) specific object instances. If a particular person, Rina, works for a particular company, Acolyte Systems, then a link exists between those two exact objects — not between the classes Person and Company in general.
Association An association is the description of a group of links that share common structure and meaning. Where a link connects two objects, an association connects two classes. "A Person works for a Company" is an association; every individual employment relationship — Rina at Acolyte, Sam at Blythe Corp — is a link that is an instance of that association.

The relationship between a link and an association is exactly the relationship between an object and a class: a link is an instance of an association, in the same way an object is an instance of a class. This is why associations are drawn once on a class diagram but may be realized as many links on an object diagram.

Fig. 3.2.1 — One association on the class diagram (left) generalizes many links between actual objects (right).

Notation

  • An association is drawn as a solid line between two class boxes.
  • The association may carry a name, usually a verb phrase, sometimes with a small triangle indicating the direction it should be read.
  • Each end of the line is a role — the way the class at the other end is seen from this end (e.g. from Company's side, a Person plays the role of "employee").
  • A line ending in an arrowhead instead of a plain end indicates navigability — that the association can be traversed efficiently in that direction (e.g. a Company object can be asked for its employees, but not necessarily the reverse).
  • An association may itself have attributes when the data belongs to the relationship rather than to either class — for example, a start-date belongs to the employment, not to the Person or the Company alone. Such attributes are attached to the association line, sometimes via an association class.
Fig. 3.2.2 — Anatomy of an association: name, roles, navigability, and an association-class attribute.
A link is not merely a "pointer" — it is a semantic fact about the domain. Whether it is later implemented as a pointer, a foreign key, or a join table is an implementation decision made much later, in design, not in analysis.
3.2.2

Multiplicity

Multiplicity states how many objects on one end of an association may be linked to a single object on the other end, at any given moment. It is written as a number or range placed near the class end it constrains, and it is one of the most important pieces of information an association carries — it directly shapes how the relationship will later be implemented (a single reference versus a collection).

NotationMeaningTypical example
1Exactly oneEvery Order is placed by exactly one Customer
0..1Zero or one (optional, single)A Person has at most one current Passport
0..* (or *)Zero or moreA Customer may place zero or more Orders
1..*One or more (at least one)An Order must contain one or more LineItems
3..7A bounded rangeA Committee has 3 to 7 Members

Reading a multiplicity pair

Multiplicity is always read from the perspective of a single object at the opposite end. The number placed next to Company answers: "for one Person, how many Companies?" — not the reverse. Mixing this up is the single most common modeling error involving associations.

1-to-1 1-to-many many-to-many
Fig. 3.2.3 — The three structural shapes of an association, distinguished purely by multiplicity.

One-to-one

Each object on either side corresponds to exactly one on the other. Example: a Person and their current Passport. These are relatively rare in practice, since most real relationships are more flexible.

One-to-many

The most common shape. One Company links to many Employees, but each Employee links back to only one Company. This is what eventually becomes a collection attribute on one side and a single reference on the other.

Many-to-many

Objects on both sides may be linked to several objects on the other side — e.g. Students and Courses. Many-to-many associations often carry their own attributes (like a grade) and are frequently resolved into an explicit association class during design.

Optional vs mandatory

Whether a "0" is allowed at the low end of the range distinguishes an optional participant from a mandatory one — e.g. a Book may exist with 0..1 reviews, but an Order requires 1..* line items to be valid at all.

3.2.3

Importance of Associations

Associations are not a cosmetic addition to a class diagram — they capture as much meaning about a domain as the classes themselves. A model made only of classes, with no associations, says nothing about how the domain actually behaves.

  • They record structural facts about the domain. Before any code is written, an association like "a Policy insures a Vehicle" states a rule of the business itself, independent of any particular implementation.
  • They define access paths. An association tells you which objects can be reached from which — given a Customer, can you get to their Orders? This determines what queries and navigations the eventual system must support.
  • They drive implementation structure. Multiplicity on an association is what later becomes a single attribute reference (multiplicity of 1) or a collection — a list, set, or map (multiplicity of many). Getting the association right in analysis prevents costly restructuring in design.
  • They surface hidden concepts. When data or behavior clearly belongs to the relationship rather than to either class alone (like a start-date of employment, or a grade for an enrollment), the association reveals the need for an association class — a concept easy to miss if only classes are considered.
  • They constrain integrity. Multiplicity such as 1..* versus 0..* encodes business rules directly into the model — e.g. an Order without at least one LineItem is not a valid order — which later becomes a validation rule in the system.
  • They support incremental discovery. Associations are usually found early, in general terms, and refined over successive passes: first "Person works for Company," then role names, then multiplicity, then whether the relationship needs its own attributes. This mirrors how understanding of a real domain typically deepens.
Fig. 3.2.4 — An association is the seed from which roles, multiplicity, navigability, and — where needed — an association class all grow.

Takeaway

A link is a fact about two specific objects; an association is the general rule that produces many such facts. Multiplicity tells you the shape of that rule, and together these ideas turn a set of isolated classes into a connected, navigable model of the domain — the structural backbone the rest of the design builds on.
